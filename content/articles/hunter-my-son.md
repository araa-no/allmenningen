## Part I: The Echo in the Hallway

You know, folks often ask me what the hardest part of this job is. They expect me to talk about the maps. The big maps in the Situation Room, with the pins and the colors. Red for trouble, blue for our guys. They think it’s the briefings at 6:00 AM, when the sun is just barely creeping over the Delaware, and the generals are speaking in a language that is very precise, but doesn't always make sense to a guy from Scranton.

But it isn’t the maps. It’s the hallways.

The White House has these incredibly long hallways. High ceilings. You walk down them, and your shoes make a sound on the floor. Click. Clack. Click. Clack. It’s a rhythm that gets into your head. And when you walk down these hallways alone—because the Secret Service, God love ‘em, they hang back just a step, they’re ghosts, professional ghosts—you hear an echo.

I hear his echo.

Hunter. My son.

People see the name in the papers. They see the headlines, the noise, the chatter on the television screens that are omnipresent in this town. They see a caricature. A punchline for the late-night fellows. But they don’t know the boy who used to ride his bike too fast down the driveway in Wilmington. They don’t know the kid who had a mop of blond hair that stuck up in every direction, no matter how much water his mother put on it. Neilia. She’d laugh. She’d say, "Joey, he looks like a scarecrow." And we’d laugh, because back then, the world was a place where you could just laugh at a boy's messy hair.

I miss him. I miss the weight of his hand in mine when he was small. His hand was sticky, usually. Always an apple juice box or a Popsicle. The sticky hands of summer. Now, when I shake hands with world leaders—Kings and Prime Ministers, powerful men with firm grips—I am always, secretly, looking for that sticky little hand. I am looking for Hunter.

I am old. Let’s be frank. The numbers on the birthday cake don't lie, even if I try to ignore them. And when you are old, the fatigue isn't just in the knees or the back. It’s a heaviness in the soul. It’s knowing that the clock is ticking louder than it used to. And when you are tired, the barriers you build—the ones that keep the grief at bay, the ones that let you stand at a podium and talk about resilience—they get thin. They become like wet paper.

I walk down the hall, and I don't just hear the echo of his footsteps. I hear the sound of the front door closing in 1987, when he was a teenager, angry at me for being on the road all the time. I hear the sound of a car engine in 1972, which is a sound no father should ever have to hear replaying in his head. The echo in the hallway is a symphony of loss, and the conductor is time. And time, as I have learned, is a merciless SOB.

## Part II: The Unraveling of the Thread

They say grief is a thread. I’ve said that myself, standing at podiums, looking out at rows of cameras with blinking red lights. "Grief is a thread," I'd say. "It ties you to the people you love." It’s a good line. Poetic. I probably read it in a book by some Irish poet, or maybe an Irish cop told me once at a wake in South Philly.

But here is the thing about threads. If you pull on one end, the whole sweater can come apart. And Hunter… Hunter pulled on the thread.

After Beau died. My beautiful Beau. The lion of the family, the one who did everything right, the prosecutor, the soldier, the man who kept his room clean and his shoes polished. When the cancer took Beau, the thread snapped. It didn't just unravel; it snapped with a sound like a rifle shot in the woods.

I was falling. I didn't know it then, but I was falling down a deep, dark well. And Hunter, my surviving son, the one who carried the broken pieces of my heart in his hands—he fell with me. Only, he fell faster. He didn't hit the bottom; he found a way to keep falling sideways. Into the shadows. Into the places where the light doesn't reach, where the chemicals swirl in the air and the eyes of the people are glassy and distant.

I miss the Hunter who was on the upswing. For a while, after the first time he got sober, he was magnificent. He wrote a book. He stood on stages and told his story to help other people. He looked me in the eye, and I saw the boy from Scranton again. The one with the sticky hands. I saw the resilience. I thought, "This is it. We made it to the other side of the river."

But the river is wider than you think. And the currents are tricky.

I read the reports. They put them on my desk in a blue folder. Top secret, or confidential, or whatever the classification is for a father's heartbreak. They tell me things. Business dealings. Laptops. Pictures. It’s a labyrinth of mistakes. A web spun by a man who was running from a bear that was only in his own mind.

I don't understand all of it. The financial stuff, the foreign entanglements, the intricacies of corporate board seats in places I can barely pronounce on a good day. My mind, it doesn't track like it used to. The synapses, as the doctors call them—they fire a little slower now. Sometimes I reach for a word, a simple word like "calendar" or "bicycle," and it’s hiding behind a curtain in my brain. So how am I supposed to understand the complex, tangled web of my son's attempts to find his worth in a world that told him he was only worth his last name?

I miss him because he is right there, in the next room, or on the other end of a phone line, but he is behind a wall of his own making. A wall made of pride and shame and the ghost of his dead brother.

## Part III: The Fog and the Freight Train

You hear stories. From friends. From aides who whisper when they think I’m not listening, though my hearing is better than they think—it’s the processing that takes a minute. They tell me about the dinners. The dinners where he isn't there. The empty chair at the table in Delaware. The holidays.

Thanksgiving is the worst. The smell of the turkey. The cranberry sauce. Beau used to make the cranberry sauce. He had a secret ingredient. Orange zest. I keep asking Jill, "What was that thing Beau put in the sauce?" And Jill looks at me with those sad, beautiful eyes, and she says, "Orange zest, Joe." And I nod, but I forget again five minutes later.

When you have dementia—I don't know if they call it that officially, they have a lot of fancy words, cognitive decline, whatever—but when your mind starts to leave the station without you, the present moment becomes a very slippery fish. You hold it, and it wiggles out of your hands.

But the past. The past is cast in concrete.

I am standing in the kitchen, and it is 1975. I am holding Hunter. He is crying because he scraped his knee. I am putting a Band-Aid on it. The Band-Aid has superheroes on it. I am telling him, "You're tough. You're a Biden." And he stops crying. He believes me.

Then the freight train of the present hits me. I am in the White House. I am the President of the United States of America. And my son is in a courtroom in Delaware. Or he is in a house in California. Or he is painting pictures. He paints beautiful pictures, actually. Abstract things. But they are full of dark reds and angry blacks. They look like bruises.

I want to pick up the phone. I want to call him and say, "Hunter, come home. Let's have a sandwich. Let's sit on the porch and watch the fireflies." But I am the President. And the phone in my pocket is a recording device. The walls have ears. The airwaves are electric with malice. If I call him, it becomes a headline. "President Intervenes in Son's Legal Troubles." It becomes a conspiracy. It becomes another arrow in the back of the democracy I have spent fifty years trying to protect.

So I don't call. And the silence is deafening.

It is a profound loneliness, being the father of a man who is drowning while you are standing on the shore, and the whole world is watching to see if you throw a life raft or a stone. You want to scream at them: "He is my boy! He is sick! He is hurting!" But you can't. You have to stand there with your jaw set, looking Presidential, while your insides are screaming.

I am so tired. My bones ache. My soul aches. I miss the sound of his laugh. It was a loud laugh. Boisterous. It filled up a room. Now the rooms I sit in are filled with the hum of air conditioners and the scratching of pens. It is a sterile world. A world without sticky hands.

## Part IV: Don't Go That Way

If I could talk to him. If I could just sit with him, man to man, not President to defendant, not father to prodigal son, but just two Irish guys at a bar, drinking a draft beer that is too cold, staring at the wood grain on the table. If I could do that, I know exactly what I would say.

I would say, "Kid. Don't go that way."

I don't even know what "that way" means. And that's the truth. I am a man who has been in the Senate, I have been Vice President, I have negotiated with despots and dictators. I can talk for three hours straight about infrastructure policy. I can filibuster with the best of them. But when I try to define the danger my son is walking into, the words fail me.

"That way."

Is it the way of the bottle? The powder? The pills? We've been down that road before. We know the signs. The empty eyes, the slurred speech, the disappearing acts. We know the geography of addiction like we know the roads from Wilmington to Washington.

But this feels different. This feels deeper. More structural.

Maybe "that way" means the way of the grifters. The people who whisper in his ear. "You're a Biden. You have value. They want access to your father. Sell it." They are vultures. They circle around damaged men. They pick at the carcass of his self-esteem. They tell him he is smart, he is a player, he is a big deal. And a man who has lost his brother, who feels like he is the spare part, the one who didn't die but maybe should have—he listens to the vultures. Because their voices are the only ones saying he matters.

I want to grab him by the collar. Not hard. Just firm. Like my father did to me once when I was acting up in high school. I want to look at him and say, "Don't go that way. That way leads to a place where I cannot follow. That way leads to the destruction of the name you carry. Not my name. Your name. Hunter. You are a hunter. Don't hunt in the dark."

But I can't be sure he would hear me.

The dementia—the fog—it isn't just in my head. It's in the air around my family. We speak in code. We walk on eggshells. We pretend everything is fine. "How are you, Dad?" "I'm good, Hunter. How are the girls?" "They're great, Dad." And we hang up, and we both know we just told each other a beautiful, polite lie.

"Don't go that way." It sounds like a warning about a physical direction. Like turning left instead of right at the traffic light. But it is a warning about the soul. It is a warning about the corrosion of character in a town—Washington—that eats character for breakfast.

I worry that he has gone so far down that path that the light behind him is just a pinprick. I worry that the chemicals in his brain, the ones he put there and the ones that are just genetic bad luck, have rewritten his map. He thinks he is heading towards the treasure, but he is heading towards the cliff.

## Part V: The Sticky Hands and the Setting Sun

The sun is going down. I can see it from the window here. The Resolute Desk is big and solid, but the light from the sun makes the dust particles dance in the air. They look like tiny golden bugs. They float. They have no direction. They just go where the wind pushes them.

I am tired. I am so incredibly tired. The kind of tired that sleep doesn't fix.

Being President is a young man's game, or at least a middle-aged man's game. You need a mind that clicks like a Swiss watch. Mine clicks like an old screen door on a rusty hinge. Sometimes it sticks. Sometimes it slams shut when you don't want it to. I lose the thread of the story. I forget the name of the Mayor of a city I've visited twelve times. I stand on stages and I reach for a word, and it’s not there, and I have to pivot, I have to improvise, I have to rely on fifty years of muscle memory to get me through the paragraph.

And through all of that, the only thing I can see clearly—sharper than the teleprompter, sharper than the faces of the Cabinet members—is Hunter.

I don't care about the politics anymore. Let the historians figure it out. Let them write their books about the infrastructure bill or the foreign policy. Let them debate my legacy. My legacy is not in this office. My legacy is in a house in Delaware, and it is fractured. It is broken.

I miss him so much it makes my teeth hurt. It is a physical ache in the jaw.

I just want him to be okay. I want him to find a quiet room. A room with no cameras, no lawyers, no art dealers, no laptops. A room with a window looking out at a tree. I want him to sit in that room and realize that he doesn't need to prove anything. He doesn't need to make a deal. He doesn't need to be the son of the President. He just needs to be Hunter.

Come back, kid.

Please, just come back from wherever you are.

Don't go that way. Turn around. The fog is rolling in, and it's getting dark, and I am too old to chase you anymore. I don't have the legs for it. I don't have the mind for it. But I have the love for it. I have an endless, stupid, stubborn Irish love for it.

The dust is settling on the desk now. The sun is gone. The hallway is dark. I have to walk back to the residence now. Up the stairs. One foot in front of the other. Click. Clack.

Maybe tomorrow I will call him. Maybe tomorrow I will risk the headlines. Maybe tomorrow I will just say, "I love you, Hunter. Bring the girls over. We'll have sandwiches." And maybe, just maybe, he won't go that way.

But tonight, the house is quiet. And the echo is very, very loud.
