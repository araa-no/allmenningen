In the spring of 2024, residents of a mid-sized city in southern Spain did something quietly radical. They didn't protest, didn't lobby, didn't write op-eds. Instead, they **simply took over** an abandoned industrial site on the outskirts of town and turned it into a community solar farm — one that now provides free electricity to three hundred households and a local school.

No private company owned it. No government agency managed it. The installation was planned, funded, and maintained by the neighborhood itself, through an assembly that meets every second Tuesday in the basement of a former textile mill. The project cost €420,000 — raised through a combination of municipal grants, crowdfunding, and a low-interest loan from a regional credit union.

## The Market Isn't Going to Save Us
For the better part of four decades, the dominant political imagination in the West has been shaped by a simple premise: *if we get the prices right, the market will allocate resources efficiently.* This idea — sometimes called neoliberalism, sometimes called "the Washington Consensus," sometimes just called common sense — has been extraordinarily powerful, and extraordinarily wrong.

The failures are well documented by now. *Markets don't price externalities.* They don't account for the carbon a factory pumps into the atmosphere, or the loneliness that follows the closure of a town's only grocery store, or the democratic deficit that emerges when essential services are run by distant shareholders rather than the people who depend on them. These aren't fringe observations. They're the basic conclusions of half a century of environmental economics, behavioral research, and — perhaps most importantly — the lived experience of hundreds of millions of people.

>"The commons is not a relic of the pre-industrial past. It is a framework for the post-industrial future."

### What Is the Commons, Exactly?
The term is older than capitalism itself. In medieval England, commons referred to pastures, forests, and waterways that were collectively managed by local communities — not owned by the crown, not parceled out to lords, but held in shared stewardship. The economist Elinor Ostrom, who won the Nobel Prize in 2009 for her work on the subject, showed that such arrangements were not romantic exceptions but **robust, self-governing systems** that often outperformed both state-run and privately run alternatives.

Today's commons look different, but the logic is the same. Wikipedia is a commons. Open-source software is a commons. Community land trusts, municipal broadband networks, cooperative housing, and public libraries are all commons — resources that are neither state property nor private property, but something else entirely: shared infrastructure governed by the people who use it.

### From Theory to Practice
What's changing now is scale. In Barcelona, the city government has actively supported the creation of over two dozen digital commons — platforms for everything from ride-sharing to mutual aid coordination — that are owned and governed by their users rather than by venture capitalists. In Cleveland, a network of worker cooperatives anchored by the Evergreen Cooperatives has demonstrated that it's possible to build a local economy that doesn't export its profits to distant investors. In India, the Deccan Development Society has helped thousands of Dalit women establish community-managed seed banks that preserve biodiversity while ensuring food sovereignty.

These projects don't share a single ideology. Some are explicitly socialist, others are pragmatically localist, still others are rooted in Indigenous traditions of collective stewardship. What they share is a basic conviction: that people are capable of governing the resources they depend on, and that doing so tends to produce more equitable, more resilient, and more ecologically sane outcomes than either the market or the state can deliver alone.

The question is no longer whether the commons works. The evidence is overwhelming. The question is whether we have the political imagination — and the political will — [to let it grow](https://google.com).
